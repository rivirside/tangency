Book Review: Freedom is a Constant Struggle

Ferguson, Palestine, and the Foundations of a Movement | Published on June 30, 2025

If Saul Alinsky's *Rules for Radicals* is the tactical field manual for community organizing, then Angela Y. Davis's *Freedom is a Constant Struggle* is its philosophical and ethical compass. This slim but profound book, a collection of speeches, interviews, and essays, argues that the most important work of an activist is to see the connections—between the local and the global, the past and the present, and between seemingly disparate movements. Davis challenges us to move beyond single-issue politics and embrace a truly intersectional and internationalist vision of liberation. It is a foundational text for any activist seeking to build a movement that is not only powerful, but also principled and just.

Book Summary: The Power of Connection

Davis's central thesis is that struggles against state violence, racism, and oppression cannot be understood in isolation. The book powerfully weaves together the Black freedom struggle in the United States, particularly the rise of the Black Lives Matter movement after the murder of Michael Brown in Ferguson, with the Palestinian struggle against Israeli occupation. She argues that these are not separate fights, but different fronts in a global battle against a system of power that relies on racism, colonialism, and militarism. Davis contends that a key element of modern activism literacy is the ability to recognize these connections and build solidarity across borders and identities.

Key Themes: An Intersectional Framework

The book is organized around a few core themes that function more like invitations than checklists. Intersectionality as a lived practice means understanding that movements must be feminist, queer-affirming, and anti-capitalist all at once because oppression is braided together. Abolitionist feminism reframes public safety as abundance—housing, care, and education that make cages unnecessary. And internationalism reminds us that Ferguson and Palestine are bound together; when we learn from each other’s tactics, our local fights become part of a shared global struggle.

How to Leverage for Effective Change Today

Davis’s work reads like a coach whispering from the sidelines. She urges us to build deliberate coalitions—pair your climate campaign with a Black liberation organization, for example, so environmental justice stays rooted in racial justice. She prods us to hold an abolitionist lens up to every tactic: are we tweaking the system or creating something altogether different? And she reminds us to practice global solidarity by amplifying comrades abroad and joining their days of action so that none of us feels isolated in the fight.

The Bottom Line

*Freedom is a Constant Struggle* is less a "how-to" guide than a "why-to" and "what-if" guide. It challenges activists to think more deeply, connect more broadly, and imagine more radically. While Alinsky provides the tools to take on a single target, Davis provides the worldview to take on the entire system. It is an essential text that provides the ethical and intellectual foundation needed to ensure that the world we build in the aftermath of our victories is truly just, equitable, and liberated for all.
