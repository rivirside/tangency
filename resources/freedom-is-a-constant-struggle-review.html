Book Review: Freedom is a Constant Struggle

Ferguson, Palestine, and the Foundations of a Movement | Published on June 30, 2025

If Saul Alinsky's *Rules for Radicals* is the tactical field manual for community organizing, then Angela Y. Davis's *Freedom is a Constant Struggle* is its philosophical and ethical compass. This slim but profound book, a collection of speeches, interviews, and essays, argues that the most important work of an activist is to see the connectionsâ€”between the local and the global, the past and the present, and between seemingly disparate movements. Davis challenges us to move beyond single-issue politics and embrace a truly intersectional and internationalist vision of liberation. It is a foundational text for any activist seeking to build a movement that is not only powerful, but also principled and just.

Book Summary: The Power of Connection

Davis's central thesis is that struggles against state violence, racism, and oppression cannot be understood in isolation. The book powerfully weaves together the Black freedom struggle in the United States, particularly the rise of the Black Lives Matter movement after the murder of Michael Brown in Ferguson, with the Palestinian struggle against Israeli occupation. She argues that these are not separate fights, but different fronts in a global battle against a system of power that relies on racism, colonialism, and militarism. Davis contends that a key element of modern activism literacy is the ability to recognize these connections and build solidarity across borders and identities.

Key Themes: An Intersectional Framework

The book is organized around several core themes that provide a blueprint for a more connected and impactful form of activism.

  • Intersectionality as a Lived Practice

    For Davis, intersectionality is not just an academic buzzword; it is a vital organizing principle. She demonstrates how the forces of racism, patriarchy, and capitalism are mutually reinforcing. Therefore, a movement to end police violence must also be a feminist movement, because state violence disproportionately affects women of color. A struggle for LGBTQ+ rights must also challenge the prison-industrial complex. Davis urges activists to understand that their liberation is fundamentally bound up with the liberation of all oppressed peoples.

  • Abolitionist Feminism

    Davis is a leading advocate for prison abolition, and this book clarifies what that truly means. It is not simply about tearing down prisons; it is about abolishing the systems that make prisons seem like a solution in the first place. An "abolitionist feminist" approach means working to create a society with housing, healthcare, education, and mental health resources so robust that prisons become obsolete. It is a call to imagine and build new forms of justice that are not based on punishment and state violence, but on community care and restoration.

  • The Importance of Internationalism

    Throughout the book, Davis emphasizes the need for a global perspective. She highlights how activists in Ferguson learned from the tactics of Palestinian protestors, and how both groups recognized their shared experience of facing a militarized state. By fostering this international solidarity, movements can share strategies, build collective power, and remind themselves that they are part of a worldwide community of resistance. It transforms a local protest into a part of a global struggle for human rights.

How to Leverage for Effective Change Today

Davis's work provides a clear and actionable framework for building more powerful and ethical movements.

  • Build Deliberate Coalitions: Actively seek out partnerships with groups working on different issues. If you are an environmental group, partner with a racial justice organization to work on the issue of environmental racism. This broadens your base and strengthens your analysis.
  • Adopt an Abolitionist Lens: When tackling a problem, ask if your proposed solution is "reformist" (tinkering with a broken system) or "abolitionist" (working to create a new system entirely). This encourages a more radical and transformative approach to social change.
  • Practice Global Solidarity: Use your platform to amplify the voices of movements around the world. Educate your members about international struggles that are connected to your own, and participate in global days of action.

The Bottom Line

*Freedom is a Constant Struggle* is less a "how-to" guide than a "why-to" and "what-if" guide. It challenges activists to think more deeply, connect more broadly, and imagine more radically. While Alinsky provides the tools to take on a single target, Davis provides the worldview to take on the entire system. It is an essential text that provides the ethical and intellectual foundation needed to ensure that the world we build in the aftermath of our victories is truly just, equitable, and liberated for all.
